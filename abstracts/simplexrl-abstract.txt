The simplex algorithm remains one of the most widely used methods for solving linear programs, yet its performance depends critically on the pivot rule used to select entering variables at each iteration. Classical rules such as Dantzig's rule, Devex, and steepest edge are fixed heuristics that do not adapt to problem structure. Using an A* search over the simplex pivot graph, we show that the shortest pivot path is typically 2-4x shorter than what classical rules achieve, and that a small neighborhood around any classical rule already contains paths competitive with the global optimum. We introduce SimplexRL, a foundation model for pivot selection that generalizes across LP problem types and sizes, in contrast to prior pivot-learning methods restricted to single LP families. SimplexRL uses a hybrid GNN-transformer model architecture that caches problem encodings, enabling efficient per-pivot updates. The training process begins with an offline pretraining phase that distills demonstrations and graph search information from A*, though this is limited by the high computational cost of searching the pivot graph with a classical method. We introduce Sampled Gumbel Tree Search (SGTS) as the main online training, which plans in the real solver via the policy, value, and Q heads, and supervises training with search-improved policy iteration and value targets in the style of Gumbel MuZero. We find that policy-gradient and actor-critic methods struggle with the long-horizon credit assignment of simplex pivoting even with reward shaping, while SGTS exploits the determinism and cheap simulation of the solver to deliver substantially shorter pivot paths.
